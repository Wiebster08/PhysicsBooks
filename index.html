Classical Mechanics

Undergraduate: Classical Mechanics John R. Taylor

Classical Mechanics by John R. Taylor is commonly used by universities to teach their students classical mechanics, and for good reason. This book contains explanations of unusual clarity and a very good set of problems.

Graduate: Classical Mechanics by H. Goldstein

Classical Mechanics by H. Goldstein is the standard textbook for graduate courses in classical mechanics. This book covers all classical mechanics topics in great detail, including Lagrangian and Hamiltonian mechanics. Don't be deceived by the same name as the previous book, you will find this one to be much more advanced.

Electrodynamics

Undergraduate: Classical Mechanics John R. Taylor

A clear, friendly, undergraduate-level journey through electrostatics, magnetostatics, Maxwell’s equations, waves, radiation, and a dash of relativity. It builds intuition step by step, with clean explanations and very approachable math. Ideal as a first serious dive into E&M.

Graduate: Classical Mechanics by H. Goldstein

The classic graduate-level textbook: rigorous, dense, and comprehensive. Covers boundary-value problems, radiation, relativistic fields, waveguides, scattering, and much more. Problems are formidable, but mastering them gives you professional-level command of electrodynamics.

Thermodynamics and Statistical Mechanics

Undergraduate: Thermodynamics and an Introduction to Thermostatistics by H. Callen

Callen develops thermodynamics from a handful of precise postulates, turning the subject into a clear, logically sculpted framework. It then bridges seamlessly into statistical mechanics, tying microscopic ensembles to macroscopic laws with real elegance. The result is a text that’s concise, rigorous, and perfect for anyone who wants thermodynamics with conceptual depth rather than rote formulas.

Graduate: Fundamentals of Statistical and Thermal Physics by F. Reif

A graduate-level classic that builds statistical mechanics from microscopic principles with clarity and depth. Reif connects probability, ensembles, and kinetic theory to real thermodynamic behavior, covering equilibrium, quantum gases, fluctuations, and transport. It’s a rigorous, comprehensive launchpad for advanced study in statistical physics.

Waves & Optics

Undergraduate: Optics by Eugene Hecht

Optics by Eugene Hecht is a clear, comprehensive introduction to geometrical and wave optics, widely used in undergraduate physics. It explains the core principles cleanly, includes many worked examples, and offers a solid foundation for further study in modern optical science. A reliable choice if you want one book that covers the essentials well.

Graduate: Statistical Optics by Joseph W. Goodman

Statistical Optics by Joseph Goodman is a rigorous, versatile introduction to coherence, fluctuations, and the probabilistic behavior of optical fields. It builds the mathematical framework carefully and connects it directly to real optical systems. A strong choice if you want a deeper, more analytical understanding of how light behaves in practical and advanced settings.

Quantum Mechanics

It must be said that there is no definitive best book for quantum Mechanics, this holds true for other subjects as well! The best approach would be to study from multiple sources. Nevertheless here are two books I would recommend.

Undergraduate: Introduction to Quantum Mechanics by D.J. Griffiths

Griffiths' Introduction to Quantum Mechanics is a widely used undergraduate textbook that provides a clear and accessible introduction to the fundamental concepts of quantum mechanics. It covers topics such as wave-particle duality, the Schrödinger equation, and quantum states, with numerous examples and problems to reinforce understanding.

Graduate: Principles of Quantum Mechanics by R. Shankar

Shankar's Principles of Quantum Mechanics is a comprehensive graduate-level textbook that delves deeply into the mathematical foundations and advanced topics of quantum mechanics. It covers angular momentum, perturbation theory, scattering theory, and more, providing a rigorous treatment suitable for students pursuing advanced studies in quantum physics.

Special/General Relativity

Special Relativity: Spacetime Physics, by Taylor and Wheeler

ChatGPT said: Spacetime Physics is a clear, intuitive introduction to special relativity that uses geometry and thought experiments to show how space and time merge into a single spacetime structure. It focuses on concepts, diagrams, and physical insight rather than heavy math. A great choice if you want to grasp the core ideas of relativity without getting bogged down in equations.

General Relativity: Gravity: An Introduction to Einstein's General Relativity by James Hartle

Hartle’s Introduction to Einstein’s General Relativity is a concept-driven tour of gravity as curved spacetime, using physical intuition, real astrophysical examples, and gentle mathematics to build GR from the ground up. It focuses on understanding phenomena—black holes, cosmology, gravitational waves—before diving into the formal machinery. I highly recommend this book in contrast to Schutz his book. There are better books out there by now, like this book from 2021.

Nuclear and Particle Physics

Undergraduate: Introductory Nuclear Physics by Kenneth S. Krane

Krane's Introductory Nuclear Physics is a widely used undergraduate textbook that provides a comprehensive introduction to the principles and applications of nuclear physics. It covers topics such as nuclear structure, radioactivity, nuclear reactions, and applications in medicine and energy, with clear explanations and numerous examples.

Graduate: Introduction to Elementary Particles by David J. Griffiths

Griffiths' Introduction to Elementary Particles is a well-regarded high level undergraduate textbook that can be used as an graduate textbook. It offers a clear and accessible overview of particle physics. It covers the fundamental particles and forces, quantum field theory, symmetries, and the Standard Model, providing a solid foundation for students pursuing advanced studies in particle physics.

Econophysics

For every level: Econophysics: An Introduction by Sitabhra Sinha

A compact and accessible introduction to how statistical physics tools can be applied to financial and economic systems. Sinha and co-authors present core ideas—like power laws, market fluctuations, and agent behavior—clearly and with minimal prerequisites. A concise, useful starting point for anyone curious about the physics–economics crossover.

Astrophysics

Introductory book 1: An Introduction to Modern Astrophysics by Bradley W. and Carroll D. Ostlie

Krane's Introductory Nuclear Physics is a widely used undergraduate textbook that provides a comprehensive introduction to the principles and applications of nuclear physics. It covers topics such as nuclear structure, radioactivity, nuclear reactions, and applications in medicine and energy, with clear explanations and numerous examples.

Introductory book 2: The Physical Universe: An Introduction to Astronomy -Frank Shu

“The Physical Universe” by Frank Shu is a classic, somewhat older book that remains excellent: it gives a clear, elegant overview of fundamental physics, valued for its conceptual depth and lasting insight despite predating many modern developments. A real hidden gem that can help you with your studies.

Biophysics

Biological Physics: Energy, Information, Life by Philip Nelson

“Biological Physics: Energy, Information, Life” by Philip Nelson is a highly regarded modern classic that offers a lucid, conceptually rich introduction to how physical principles such as energy, entropy, and information shape biological systems, celebrated for its depth, clarity, and elegance. The author is an award‑winning physicist and educator, including receiving the Emily M. Gray Award for outstanding contributions to biophysics education.

Atmospheric Physics

Atmosphere-Ocean Dynamics, by Adrian E. Gill

“Atmosphere‑Ocean Dynamics” by Adrian E. Gill is a classic, highly respected book that systematically explains the physics of large‑scale atmospheric and oceanic flows. Praised for its clarity, rigor, and insightful use of models, it remains a cornerstone in geophysical fluid dynamics. Adrian E. Gill was an award‑winning meteorologist and oceanographer, honored with a prize named after him for his lasting contributions.

Programming

A Student’s Guide to Python for Physical Modeling by Jesse M. Kinder & Philip Nelson

This book introduces Python programming with a focus on physics applications. It guides students from the basics of coding to advanced modeling and simulation, using clear examples from mechanics, electromagnetism, and other physical systems. Ideal for learning Python while directly applying it to real-world physics problems. Since python is the most popular programming language in science right now, this is a great book to start with.

Computational Physics by Nicholas J. Giordano & Hisao Nakanishi

This book provides a thorough introduction to computational methods for solving physics problems. It covers numerical algorithms, simulations, and modeling techniques applied across mechanics, electromagnetism, quantum physics, and statistical mechanics. The focus is on developing computational thinking and problem-solving skills, making it an essential resource for learning how to approach physics with modern computational tools.

Math

“Mathematical Methods for Physics and Engineering: A Comprehensive Guide” by K. F. Riley, M. P. Hobson & S. J. Bence

This book is a truly comprehensive reference, covering just about every mathematical method a physics or engineering student might need. From calculus and linear algebra to differential equations, complex analysis, and special functions, it combines clear explanations with practical examples, making it an indispensable guide for applying mathematics to real-world physics problems.

“Mathematics for Physics: A Guided Tour for Graduate Students” by Michael Stone & Paul Goldbart

This book is a hidden gem for physics students seeking a deep yet intuitive understanding of the mathematics underlying modern physics. It guides readers through advanced topics like group theory, differential geometry, and linear algebra, always connecting the mathematics directly to physical concepts. Its clear explanations and thoughtful examples make it an ideal bridge between abstract math and real-world physics applications.
